Electric Skateboard: Product Design (Pt. 1)

  • kkamiya
  • March 27, 2019
  • Skateboard
  • 0 Comments

Welcome to my first solo design project. For this project I will be designing and manufacturing an electric skateboard. Why? I thought it would be fun and I assume it wouldn’t take up a significant amount of time. I also wanted to have an electric skateboard without having to pay $1000 to firms. These blog posts will be broken into multiple parts to make the information digestible.

Designing and manufacturing a product takes the right kind of mindset and technical knowledge. As I have learned within the Purdue class, ME 263 (Intro to ME design, innovation, and entrepreneurship), engineering design is not a cake walk. In order to ensure success, a framework of development needs to be followed. Three steps I will be taking throughout this project are: problem definition, concept generation and evaluation, and product design. This first port will be talking exclusively about phase 1.

Phase 1: Product Design

“There is no such thing as a new idea. It is impossible. We simply take a lot of old ideas and put them into a sort of mental kaleidoscope. We give them a turn and they make new and curious combinations.” – Mark Twain

I am not here to create something new. What I am trying to do is emulate the concepts of those that came before me and improving upon them. In order to understand the direction that I would have to take, it becomes necessary to conduct market research/ benchmarking.

While the most famous company producing electric boards is Boosted Boards, one can’t ignore the competition. Here I have compiled a table of the characters each of these flagship products have.   These will give us a clear idea as to what needs to be surpassed to create a superior product.

Benchmarking specifications of Electric Skateboards

Here comes the interesting part, designing the motor and the method of drive. In order to get some insight into this portion of the project it is once again pest to look at what the competition is doing. All designs I have reviewed incorporate a belt drive system to put the board in motion. The motors that are being used are either single or dual out runner models. While there are some fringe skateboards that directly incorporate the motor into the wheel (Mellow boards), the design choice requires more processing than the regular one. These motors are then controlled by either a RC controller or a phone app.

These insights give me the ground work to get started in the project. The final design of the skateboard should incorporate a belt drive and a motor that will not stall under my weight. Now we can move on to phase 2, concept generation and evaluation.
