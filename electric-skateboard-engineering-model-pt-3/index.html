Electric Skateboard: Engineering Model (pt. 3)

  • kkamiya
  • May 9, 2019
  • Uncategorized
  • 0 Comments

In part 3 of this series of discussing electric skateboard design I will be moving on to the development of the engineering model and product development. Because the product is an electric skateboard, one of the most important parts going into this object is the motor. Making the wrong choice in motor will not only set me back in terms of money, but also in time and energy. Now, lets get to it.

Speed/ RPM Necessary

I want the skateboard to be as fast as the ones currently on the market. This would mean that the board would have to be able to go 30 km/hr (500 m/min) at least on a flat surface. In order to calculate the amount of RPM necessary to make this possible we must take a measurement of the wheels. We know that each wheel has a diameter of 6.2 cm.

Circumference = 2π(radius) = 0.1948 m

Rotations Necessary: Distance/ Circumference = 2567.02 rotations

We need a motor that can handle 2600 rpm. Anything higher would be fantastic.

Torque Necessary

The ideal motor cannot just have a high RPM, but it also needs to be able to exert a significant amount of torque. How much torque do we need exactly? In order to find out we need to apply concepts that I learned in dynamics and statics. The following pictures show the work that has been done. In the end it was found that the motor would need to have a max torque of 14.9 Nm. This means that a motor will need to be able to exert 14.9 Nm and 2600 rpm.

Motor of Choice

The motor being used has to fulfill the two primary criteria:

  1. RPM >= 3000
  2. Stalling torque >= 15 N/m

To find such a motor it is simply a process of elimination. Going through the common online markets I found one motor that appeared to fit my specifications. The Turnigy Aerodrive SK3 – 6374-192KV Brushless Out runner Motor seems to be the ideal choice, albeit an expensive one.  The torque curve for this model is shown below. Its honestly quite a bit more than what we need, but that would add to the overall effectiveness of the product. Two of these motors will be more than enough to provide the force necessary for the device to operate at any condition and at a high velocity.

Now that all the parts being used is clear I will begin to 3D model all of the parts using Solidworks. Part 4 of this series will be discussing the 3D modelling process.
