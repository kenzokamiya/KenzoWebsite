Electric Skateboard: Concept Generation and Evaluation (pt. 2)

  • kkamiya
  • March 30, 2019
  • Skateboard
  • 0 Comments

In the earlier blog post I discussed the first phase of the design process, benchmarking, and now I will be moving on to the second phase, concept generation and evaluation. This is personally my favorite step of the whole process. Designing and iterating is where its at. Manufacturing products is fun but a little pricier than I would like.

I went ahead and collected the skateboard that I will be converting to an electric one. Luckily, I was able to buy an old one from a friend for $25. While its smaller dimensions compared to a regular longboard is concerning, I am sure that I will be able to design around it. Let’s begin with designing the motor mount.

Designing Motor Mounts

In design it is best to iterate. By just creating bunch of different ideas through brainstorming I just find it easier to choose the best one. Here are several ideas that I have come with.

In design it is best to iterate. By just creating bunch of different ideas through brainstorming I just find it easier to choose the best one. Here are several ideas that I have come with.

Design #1

Design 1 is a motor mount that is built right into the truck. The motor will then be easily mounted on top of the machined part without any addition. Because the entire thing is machined together

 

 

Design 1 machines a completely new set of trucks where the engine can be mounted onto easily. The issue with this design would be the necessity of manufacturing the entire part by myself. Milling the device by hand would be highly impractical. Getting all the different angles necessary to create the device would be difficult. This is further exacerbated by the fact that each angle in the truck has to be exactly right in order to work effectively.  On the other hand, a custom-made set of trucks would greatly simplify assembly.

Design # 2

 

 

This design proposes to instead of making a completely new truck, the user attaches a motor mount to the truck. The mount would allow for small changes in the design without compromising the work done previously. Machining a motor mount would be simple compared to design 1. Even so, the mounting of this device will prove to be a little challenging. The typical design of a truck involves a significant amount of curves. In order to clamp down the mount there needs to be a straight edge area. Either I will have to buy a new set of matching trucks that have the design that I want or I mill down the current trucks into what I want. Milling the trucks down is not ideal because that could compromise the strength of the truck and down the line lead to critical failure/ buckling.

 

Design #3

 

 

 

Mount the object directly onto the skateboard using a custom designed motor mount. While this would simplify the mounting process significantly, I cannot be sure that the belt will stay on whenever the board turns. Just buy riding the skateboard it is clear to me that the trucks and board do not move the same amount. Therefore, the distance between the motor and the wheel will fluctuate a significant amount over time. Having the belt fall off due to this change of distance would be inevitable. Its clear that this design would need a significant amount of testing to be sure that it works consistently. I cannot see myself being able to design an effective product with this.

Decision Matrix

With several methods available to mount the motor it is now necessary to decide which one would be the best for our purposes. Of the three design choices, only two are viable. To help decide I will be using a decision matrix. A decision matrix helps users make the best decision by evaluating the strengths and weaknesses of each design. Depending on how important each part is, a different value for the weight will be assigned. Then either 1, 0, -1 is given to the product depending on how well it meets the criteria.
